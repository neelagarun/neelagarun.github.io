In my previous post, we looked at how simple rules in the Yard-Sale model (random pairings and coin flips) lead to wealth condensation. That was a closed system with synthetic rules. Real world macroeconomics is significantly messier. To move from theoretical agents to the actual economy, we must face the ultimate benchmark: the Consumer Price Index (CPI).

The challenge is high dimensional. We are looking at the **FRED-MD dataset**, a monthly database of over 120 macroeconomic variables. Predicting the seasonally adjusted CPI (**CPIAUCSL**) requires filtering through a massive amount of noise to find a predictive signal.

### Data Cleaning and Assumptions

Before any modeling begins, the data must be made usable. The FRED-MD dataset often contains missing values due to varying reporting lags. My cleaning process follows a strict protocol:

* **Handling Missingness:** Columns that are entirely empty are dropped. For remaining gaps, I use a forward fill followed by a backward fill to ensure continuity without introducing future information.
* **Temporal Alignment:** All series are sorted chronologically. To predict CPI at time $t+h$, we only use features from times $\le t$. 

A core assumption here is that the historical relationship between these 120 variables and inflation contains enough structural stability to be learned. However, as we will see, stability is a relative term in macroeconomics.

### The Stationarity Constraint

One does not simply regress on raw CPI levels. Like the energy accumulation in our Yard-Sale model, most FRED-MD series are strongly trended. If you feed raw levels into a neural network, the model will likely cheat by learning the trend, failing to capture the actual dynamics of inflation. 

To make the learning problem stationary and well scaled, I applied a first difference transform to the features. More importantly, the target is the $h$ step change in the CPI level:

$$\Delta y_{t+h} = \text{Level}_{t+h} - \text{Level}_t$$

By training on the difference, we transform a non-stationary random walk into a stationary sequence that is easier for the optimizer to navigate.

### Feature Selection: Pruning the Forest

With 121 candidate features, the risk of the curse of dimensionality is high. An LSTM with too many parameters and too little data will simply memorize the training set. My pipeline uses a three stage filter to extract the most salient signals:

1.  **Variance Threshold:** We drop near constant series where $\sigma^2 < 10^{-8}$.
2.  **Pearson Correlation:** We keep the top 60 features with the strongest absolute correlation to our target delta.
3.  **Random Forest Importance:** We use a non-linear ensemble to refine the list to the final 30 features.

All selectors are fit strictly on the training split to prevent data leakage. This ensures the features (ranging from industrial production indices to labor market shifts) have a statistical right to be there.

### Why the LSTM?

The choice of a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network is not arbitrary. A systematic review by **Slimani and Chourabi (Modeling inflation with machine learning, 2024)** highlights that LSTMs excel at capturing the persistence of inflation. Unlike standard feed forward networks, LSTMs maintain a hidden state $h_t$ that acts as a memory of the economic window (in this case, the last 24 months).

The architecture consists of two LSTM layers followed by a linear forecasting head. The state update is governed by:

$$h_t = \text{LSTM}(x_t, h_{t-1})$$

The final forecast is generated by projecting the last hidden state through a sequence of linear layers and a ReLU activation:

$$\hat{\Delta} y_{t+h} = \mathbf{W}_2 \cdot \text{ReLU}(\mathbf{W}_1 h_L + b_1) + b_2$$

### Addressing the Black Box

Critics often call LSTMs black boxes because their internal weights are difficult to interpret. We overcome this through **interpretability by design**. By using the Random Forest as a pre-selector, we gain transparency regarding which variables are driving the model. Furthermore, we use early stopping based on validation MSE to prevent the model from over optimizing on noise, which acts as a form of implicit regularization.

### Results and the Level Trap

Training stopped at Epoch 28 once the validation loss plateaued. The metrics on the test set initially look impressive:

| Metric | Delta CPIAUCSL | Level CPIAUCSL |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **RMSE** | 0.3923 | 0.3923 |
| **MAE** | 0.2975 | 0.2975 |
| **MAPE** | 139.997% | 0.125% |

We must be extremely careful with how we interpret these numbers. 

> The level plot (top left) flatters the model. This is exactly the trap I warned about in my last message. The two lines hug each other because most of level[t+h] is just level[t], which the model gets for free as the anchor. 

If you only look at the level, you would think the model is a crystal ball. But the **MAPE** of 139.997% on the delta reveals the truth: predicting the innovation in inflation is significantly harder than predicting that inflation will exist. The model knows where the price level is, but it struggles to predict the velocity of the next move.
